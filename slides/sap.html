Preparing a Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP)

A practical guide to analytical thinking, defensible methodology, and reproducible research

Harper Adams University

Based on:

Stevens et al. (2023), A template for the authoring of statistical analysis plans

Why This Session Matters

A common misconception among early-career professionals is that statistical analysis is mainly about choosing tests.

It is not.

Professional statistical work begins before analysis.

The real task is:

  • defining the analytical objective clearly
  • understanding the structure of the data
  • anticipating weaknesses and complications
  • planning diagnostics
  • defining fallback strategies
  • ensuring reproducibility and transparency

A Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP) formalises this process.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you should be able to:

  • explain the purpose of a Statistical Analysis Plan
  • distinguish between statistical procedures and analytical reasoning
  • structure a professional SAP
  • justify analytical decisions logically
  • identify assumptions associated with common analyses
  • define diagnostics and fallback strategies
  • write analysis plans that are transparent and reproducible

What is a Statistical Analysis Plan?

A Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP) is a formal document that explains:

  • what analyses will be conducted
  • how variables will be prepared
  • how assumptions will be assessed
  • how models will be evaluated
  • how missing data will be handled
  • how results will be interpreted
  • what will happen if assumptions fail

The SAP is not simply:

“We will run a t-test.”

It is a blueprint for defensible analytical decision-making.

The Core Philosophy of an SAP

Stevens et al. describe the SAP as a mechanism for:

  • rigor
  • transparency
  • reproducibility
  • objective analysis

One of the most important ideas in the paper is:

If multiple statisticians were given the dataset and the SAP, they should arrive at essentially the same analysis.

This means:

  • analytical decisions should not be vague
  • assumptions should not be implicit
  • diagnostics should be predefined
  • alternative methods should already be planned

Why SAPs Exist

Without a predefined analysis plan:

  • analysts may choose methods after seeing results
  • assumptions may be ignored
  • outliers may be removed selectively
  • transformations may be chosen opportunistically
  • statistical significance may drive decisions

This creates:

  • bias
  • irreproducibility
  • p-hacking
  • weak scientific inference

SAPs exist to reduce these risks.

Statistical Thinking vs Test Memorisation

Weak analytical thinking:

“This looks like a t-test question.”

Professional analytical thinking:

  1. What is the research aim?
  2. What are the variable types?
  3. What assumptions are required?
  4. Are those assumptions plausible?
  5. How will they be checked?
  6. What if they fail?
  7. How robust is the conclusion?

The statistical test is the final step, not the first.

The SAP Workflow

For each research question:

  1. Research aim
  2. Variables and data types
  3. Data preparation
  4. Proposed method
  5. Assumptions
  6. Diagnostics and model checks
  7. Fallback strategy
  8. Interpretation and presentation

This structure transforms analysis from:

“running statistics”

to:

defensible scientific reasoning.

Step 1 — Defining the Research Aim

A research aim should clearly define:

  • what relationship is being studied
  • what variable is the outcome
  • what variable(s) may explain variation
  • whether the goal is:
    • comparison
    • association
    • prediction
    • explanation

Weak:

“Investigate the variables.”

Better:

“Assess whether variation in the outcome differs systematically across groups.”

Why Research Aims Matter

Poorly defined aims create:

  • inappropriate methods
  • unclear interpretations
  • post-hoc reasoning
  • over-analysis

A strong research aim determines:

  • the modelling strategy
  • the assumptions
  • the interpretation framework

The entire analysis flows from the aim.

Step 2 — Understanding Variable Types

Statistical methods depend fundamentally on:

  • the outcome variable type
  • the explanatory variable type

Common variable types:

Type Examples
Continuous numeric height, weight, income
Binary yes/no, success/failure
Categorical species, region
Ordinal satisfaction scores
Count number of events

Variable structure determines the analytical family.

Why Variable Types Matter

Different variable structures imply different:

  • assumptions
  • distributions
  • models
  • interpretations

For example:

Outcome Predictor Typical analytical family
Numeric Group Group comparison
Numeric Numeric Regression
Categorical Categorical Association testing
Count Multiple predictors Count models

The logic begins with the data structure.

Step 3 — Data Preparation

Most analytical problems begin during data preparation.

An SAP should explain:

  • missing data handling
  • recoding procedures
  • filtering rules
  • outlier assessment
  • transformations
  • derived variables
  • category aggregation

A professional SAP anticipates problems before analysis.

Data Preparation is Part of Analysis

Many students treat cleaning as separate from statistics.

In reality:

  • cleaning decisions affect conclusions
  • filtering changes distributions
  • transformations alter interpretation
  • recoding affects assumptions

Therefore:

Data preparation must be transparent and justified.

Common Data Preparation Questions

Before analysis, analysts should ask:

  • Are values missing?
  • Are there impossible values?
  • Are categories inconsistent?
  • Are there duplicated observations?
  • Are variables heavily skewed?
  • Are some categories extremely sparse?
  • Are units consistent?
  • Are there influential outliers?

This is statistical reasoning.

Step 4 — Choosing the Method

A good SAP does not merely state the method.

It explains:

  • why the method matches the research aim
  • why the variable structure supports the method
  • why assumptions are plausible
  • what the model estimates

Weak:

“A regression model will be used.”

Better:

“A regression framework is appropriate because the outcome is continuous and the objective is to quantify how variation in the response changes with explanatory variables.”

Methods Must Match the Aim

Different analytical aims require different frameworks.

Comparison

Do groups differ?

Association

Are variables related?

Prediction

Can one variable estimate another?

Explanation

Does a predictor explain systematic variation after accounting for others?

Choosing a method without understanding the analytical aim is one of the most common weaknesses in student work.

Step 5 — Assumptions

Every statistical method has assumptions.

An SAP should explicitly state:

  • what assumptions exist
  • why they matter
  • how they will be assessed

A method is not automatically valid because software produced a p-value.

Common Statistical Assumptions

Independence

Observations should not influence one another.

Normality

Residuals or group distributions may need to approximate normality.

Homoscedasticity

Variance should be relatively stable.

Linearity

Relationships should follow the assumed functional form.

Absence of multicollinearity

Predictors should not be excessively correlated.

Different methods require different assumptions.

Important Insight: Assumptions Often Apply to Residuals

Students commonly misunderstand regression assumptions.

For many models:

  • assumptions do not apply directly to raw variables
  • assumptions apply to residual behaviour

Therefore diagnostics focus on:

  • residual distributions
  • residual variance
  • residual structure

This distinction is fundamental.

Step 6 — Diagnostics and Model Checks

The paper strongly emphasises predefined diagnostics.

A SAP should explain:

  • which plots will be inspected
  • which tests may be used
  • what evidence would indicate problems
  • how model adequacy will be evaluated

Diagnostics are not optional extras.

They are part of the analysis itself.

Common Diagnostic Tools

Histograms

Assess skewness and distribution shape.

Boxplots

Assess spread and potential outliers.

QQ Plots

Assess approximate normality.

Residual vs Fitted Plots

Assess heteroscedasticity and nonlinearity.

Leverage and Cook’s Distance

Identify influential observations.

Variance Inflation Factors (VIF)

Assess multicollinearity.

Why Diagnostics Matter

Without diagnostics:

  • assumptions remain unknown
  • inference may be invalid
  • confidence intervals may be misleading
  • p-values may be unreliable

Professional analysis requires evidence that the model is appropriate.

Step 7 — Fallback Strategies

One of the strongest features of a good SAP is that it anticipates failure.

An analyst should ask:

“What if my assumptions do not hold?”

Fallback strategies may include:

  • transformations
  • robust methods
  • non-parametric approaches
  • alternative covariance structures
  • category aggregation
  • sensitivity analyses

Why Fallback Strategies Matter

Weak analysis:

“Assumptions failed, so we tried something else.”

Strong analysis:

“If diagnostics indicate substantial deviation from assumptions, the following predefined alternative approach will be used.”

This reduces analytical subjectivity.

Sensitivity Analysis

The paper places major emphasis on sensitivity analysis.

Sensitivity analysis asks:

“Would the conclusion change if different reasonable assumptions were used?”

Examples:

  • alternative missing data handling
  • robust regression instead of ordinary least squares
  • transformed vs untransformed response
  • inclusion/exclusion of influential observations

Sensitivity analysis increases analytical credibility.

Missing Data is a Statistical Problem

A good SAP must explain:

  • how missing data will be identified
  • whether missingness is likely systematic
  • whether imputation will occur
  • what assumptions the imputation relies upon

Ignoring missing data is itself a modelling decision.

Interpretation is Part of the SAP

A SAP should explain:

  • how effect sizes will be interpreted
  • how uncertainty will be presented
  • how findings will be contextualised
  • what limitations may affect interpretation

Interpretation is not:

“p < 0.05 therefore significant.”

Professional interpretation focuses on:

  • magnitude
  • uncertainty
  • practical meaning
  • robustness

Statistical Significance vs Scientific Importance

A statistically significant effect may be:

  • trivial
  • practically irrelevant
  • driven by large sample size

A non-significant effect may still contain:

  • meaningful effect sizes
  • important uncertainty
  • informative trends

Good SAPs avoid over-reliance on p-values.

Reproducibility and Transparency

The paper repeatedly emphasises reproducibility.

This means:

  • analytical steps should be explicit
  • decisions should be documented
  • procedures should be repeatable
  • alternative analysts should reach similar conclusions

Reproducibility is a cornerstone of modern science.

Why SAPs Improve Scientific Quality

According to the paper, SAPs help:

  • identify design flaws early
  • improve protocols
  • reduce bias
  • reduce p-hacking
  • improve peer review
  • improve funding confidence
  • strengthen scientific credibility

SAPs are not administrative paperwork.

They are methodological safeguards.

Common Weaknesses in Student SAPs

Naming tests without justification

Ignoring assumptions

No diagnostics

No fallback strategy

No rationale for transformations

Confusing correlation with causation

Treating software output as interpretation

Overemphasis on p-values

Characteristics of Strong SAPs

Strong SAPs:

  • are logically structured
  • justify analytical choices
  • explicitly discuss assumptions
  • define diagnostics prospectively
  • anticipate analytical complications
  • explain fallback procedures
  • focus on reasoning rather than jargon

Analytical Maturity

Analytical maturity means understanding that:

  • data are imperfect
  • models are approximations
  • assumptions matter
  • diagnostics are essential
  • interpretation requires caution
  • statistical significance is not the same as truth

A strong SAP demonstrates analytical maturity.

Classify variable types.

Step 5

Define assumptions.

Define diagnostics.

Step 8

Define fallback procedures.

Step 9

Plan interpretation and reporting.

A Useful Mental Model

A weak analyst asks:

“Which test should I run?”

A strong analyst asks:

“What structure does the data have, what assumptions are required, and how can I defend the conclusions?”

That is the philosophy behind professional statistical analysis.
